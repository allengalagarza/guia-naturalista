Black Spiny-tailed Iguana (Ctenosaura similis)

Characteristics

Size: Males can reach up to 1.3 meters in length (including tail); females are smaller. Coloration: Grayish to blackish body with dark bands along the back and tail; juveniles often show greenish or yellowish tones. Distinctive features: Long, spiny tail; robust body with thick, pointed scales. Males have more pronounced dorsal crests.

Habitat and Distribution

Geographic range: From southern Mexico to northwestern Colombia. Very common in Costa Rica's dry Pacific region, including the Nicoya Peninsula. Habitat: Dry tropical forests, open areas, rocky sites, walls, ruins, and even urban zones. Prefers warm, sunny places with refuges such as crevices or holes.

Behavior

Diet: Omnivorous. Eats leaves, flowers, fruits, insects, small vertebrates, and eggs. Juveniles are more insectivorous. Activity: Diurnal and thermoregulates by basking in the sun. Very fast and agile, able to run quickly to escape predators. Territorial behavior: Males defend territories with postures, head bobs, and occasionally bites.

Reproduction

Oviparous: Females dig a nest and lay 20 to 30 eggs per season. Breeding season: Usually during the dry season. Hatching: Eggs hatch after about 90 days, and hatchlings are completely independent.

Conservation Status

IUCN status: Least Concern. It is a very adaptable and common species. Threats: Hunting in some rural areas for meat, habitat destruction, and roadkill in urbanized zones.

Where to find it?

Regions: Very common in the Nicoya Peninsula, especially in dry, open areas near beaches, houses, parks, and ruins. Recommended parks: Ostional Wildlife Refuge, Barra Honda National Park, areas around Santa Cruz, Tamarindo, and Karen Mogensen Nature Reserve.

Fun Facts

  • It is the fastest lizard in Costa Rica, reaching speeds up to 35 km/h over short distances. It has adapted to living near humans and is often seen on roofs, gardens, and rural roads. When threatened, it emits a loud hiss and can inflate its body to appear larger.
